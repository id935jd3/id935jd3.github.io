Historical/social profile: Duindorp was historically a fishing village with roots back into the 14th century. The village housed low-income fishing families and consisted mostly of small streets and small houses. In 1903 The Hague expanded its borders, making Duindorp part of the city. Between 1915 and 1930 the city of The Hague started demolishing and condemning the old houses of the village and replacing them with social housing to sanitise the neighbourhood. During the '70s and '80s work in the fishing industry greatly diminished and many families left the neighbourhood. Unemployment increased. Duindorp was designated a "problematic" neighbourhood. In 1993 The city started demolishing houses that were no longer up to code, as part of the new "Vinex" housing policy. This sparked many protest by the inhabitants who were being displaced. Most of which were families who lived there for generations. Public utilities like the swimming pool, library and the youth centre were destroyed. Around 1100 houses were demolished, to be replaced with 750 new houses of which around half were expensive owner-occupied instead of rental units. This was done by the city in co-operation with the housing corporation "Vestia". In 1998 the action committee "S.O.S. Duindorp" was founded by inhabitants and activist in an attempt to save the neighbourhood. New high-income housing is being developed in Duindorp to this day.

Huizes: In 2017, according to Centraal Bureau voor Statistiek:

  • Properties for sale 3490
